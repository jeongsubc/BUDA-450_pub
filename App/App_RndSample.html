Appendix B — Random Sample

WVU BUDA 545

B.1 Overview

Sampling is the selection of a subset or a statistical sample of individuals from within a statistical population to estimate characteristics of the whole population.

This session provides an introduction to random sampling. It explains the concepts of population and sample, and it differentiates between probability and non-probability sampling. The session also discusses probability distributions in sampling. Additionally, it introduces inverse transform sampling, explaining how random numbers from a uniform distribution can be used to generate random samples from any target probability distribution, with examples for both discrete and continuous random variables.

B.2 Sampling

B.2.1 Population vs. Sample

In any statistical analysis, including Monte Carlo simulations, we must understand the concepts of population and sample. These play a crucial role in ensuring our simulation results are meaningful and applicable to real-world scenarios.

Population: The complete set of all possible observations or outcomes in a study.

  • Example: Every single week of PC sales the store has ever experienced the past during 100 weeks.

Sample: A subset of the population used to analyze and infer conclusions about the larger group (i.e., the population).

  • Example: The store’s sales data from the past 100 weeks.

In many practices, we often obtain an estimate (e.g., the expected demand and revenue) using a sample instead of the full population.

Why not use the entire population?
  • Collecting all data may be impractical or impossible.
  • In simulations, we generate random samples to model uncertainty.

B.2.2 Types of samples

Sampling methods are broadly categorized into probability sampling and non-probability sampling techniques.

Probability Sampling (Random Sampling) is an approach to sampling that ensures that every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. There are many methods for the probability sampling, including:

  • Simple Random Sampling
    • Every individual has an equal chance of being selected.
    • Example: Drawing names from a hat or using a random number generator.
  • Systematic Sampling
    • Every k-th individual from a list after a random starting point is selected.
    • Example: Choosing every 5th student from a school roster.
  • Stratified Sampling
    • The population is divided into subgroups (strata) based on specific characteristics (e.g., age, income level), and samples are randomly taken from each group.
    • Representation of the overall population from all key subgroups is ensured.
  • Cluster Sampling
    • The population is divided into clusters (e.g., cities, schools), and entire clusters are randomly selected.
    • Used when populations are geographically spread out.

Non-Probability Sampling is another approach to sampling that do not give every individual a known or equal chance of being selected, often leading to bias. The following are some well-known methods for the non-probability sampling:

  • Convenience Sampling
    • Selecting individuals who are easy to reach.
    • Example: Surveying people at a shopping mall.
  • Judgment (Purposive) Sampling
    • The researcher selects individuals based on expertise or specific criteria.
    • Example: Interviewing only experienced professionals in an industry.
  • Quota Sampling
    • Ensuring that specific groups (e.g., gender, age) are represented, but selection within the group is non-random.
    • Example: Interviewing 50 men and 50 women without randomization.
  • Snowball Sampling
    • Existing participants recruit new participants from their network.
    • Used in hard-to-reach populations (e.g., drug users, undocumented workers).
Key Differences

A good sample should be random, unbiased, and large enough to reflect the population accurately.

Probability Sampling focuses on more representative values, and so is considered, if available, in scientific approaches.

Non-Probability Sampling is often employed due to practical issues, where it’s easier and cheaper to collect the data but more prone to bias than the probability sampling.

B.3 Random Sampling

To understand how Monte Carlo simulations work, we need to define the concept of random sampling.

B.3.1 What is a Random Sample?

A (simple) random sample is a subset of a population where every element has an equal chance of selection. This ensures that the sample accurately represents the population, reducing bias in statistical analysis and simulations.

How do we ensure a good sample for simulations?

Many stochastic simulations, including Monte Carlo simulations, employ random sampling to multiple samples that mimic real-world uncertainty, which allow us to estimate probabilities and expected values. In there, random sampling plays a critical role on:

  • Ensuring that simulation results are statistically valid.
  • Reducing bias, making the simulation accurate and reliable.
  • Forming the basis for probability estimation, leading to more realistic business decisions.

For the analysis with valid statistical properties in the result, the values in a random sample should be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.):

  • Independent: No one value should influence another.
  • Identically Distributed: Each value follows the same probability distribution as the population.

Example: A random sample from a set of five independent weekly demands selected randomly, assuming that each follows the same probability distribution as the overall demand.

B.3.2 Random Sampling with Probability Distribution

To sample random numbers fairly, a common option is a probability distribution-based approach that selects a value based on probabilities of the possible values.

Imagine a roulette wheel divided into segments proportional to demand probabilities. The value of a higher probability is assigned a larger segment on the wheel, and the value of a lower probability is assigned a smaller segment. Using such wheel, we take the value to which the wheel arrow directs as the result from spinning the wheel.

Similarly, the key idea of random sampling is to take the realization of a random variable, which is determined at random (e.g., the result from the wheel). Then, how can we make the realization at random? One idea is to label segments proportionally assigned to possible values for the realization. Then, one following question is — how can we generate such random numbers? As a fundamental tool for many modern techniques, most software from general purpose spreadsheets (e.g., rand() in Microsoft Excel) to packages in programming languages (e.g., numpy.random in python) can generate random numbers based on certain algorithms. B.4 Inverse Transform Sampling

Monte Carlo simulations often require generating random samples from a given probability distribution. Inverse Transform Sampling is a basic yet efficient method to achieve this.

B.4.1 What is Inverse Transform Sampling?

Inverse Transform Sampling is a method to generate random numbers from a specific probability distribution using uniformly distributed random numbers.

Given a cumulative distribution function (CDF) \(F_{X}(x)\), the method finds values of \(X\) such that:

\[ F_{X}(x) = r \]

where \(r\) is a uniform random number between \(0\) and \(1\) as \(r \sim \mathrm{Uniform(0,1)}\).

Here, the key idea is matching the range of the CDF’s possible values, \(0 \leq F_{X}(x) \leq 1\)) and the range of \(r\)’s possible values ( \(0 \leq r \leq 1\) ). That is, the random number of \(X\) is generated by matching its value in the CDF with a randomly generated value from \(r\).

B.4.1.1 Steps to Apply Inverse Transform Sampling

  1. Generate a uniform random number
    • Select a value \(r \sim \mathrm{Uniform(0,1)}\) that is a random number between 0 and 1.
  2. Compute the inverse CDF
    • Find the value of \(X\) such that: \[ X=F_{X}^{-1}(r) \] This step converts the uniform random number \(r\) into a value from the target distribution.
  3. Return the generated sample
    • The computed \(X\) follows the desired probability distribution.

This technique works for both discrete and continuous distributions.

B.4.3 Example of Inverse Transform Sampling with Continuous RV

Inverse Transform Sampling is also applicable to continuous RVs. Instead of mapping discrete values, we use the inverse cumulative distribution function (CDF) to generate continuous random samples.

B.4.3.1 Step 1: Defining a Continuous Random Variable

Consider a continuous RV with a probability density function (PDF) \(f_{X}(x)\):

\[ f_{X}(x)= \begin{cases} \frac{x}{8}, & 0 \leq x < 2 \\ 0, & \mathrm{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

The corresponding cumulative distribution function (CDF) is:

\[ F_{X}(x)= \begin{cases} 0, & x < 0 \\ \frac{x^2}{16} & 0 \leq x < 4 \\ 1 & x \geq 4 \end{cases} \]

B.4.4 Step 2: Applying Inverse Transform Sampling

  1. Generate a uniform random number \(r \sim \mathrm{Uniform(0,1)}\)

  2. Compute the inverse CDF

    • Solve for \(X\) using the inverse CDF \[ X = F_{X}^{-1}(r) = \sqrt{16r}. \]
    • Example:
      • If \(r=0.10\), we select \(X=1.26\) because \(0.15<0.2\).
      • If \(r=0.90\), we select \(X=3.79\) because \(0.5 \leq 0.80\).
      • If \(r=0.50\), we select \(X=2.83\) because \(0.2 \leq 0.35 < 0.5\).

  3. Return the generated sample

    • Take the selected values for \(X\) as the generated sample that follows the desired probability distribution.
    • Example:
      • The sampled \(X\) values are \(1.26\) ,\(3.79\), and \(2.83\).
